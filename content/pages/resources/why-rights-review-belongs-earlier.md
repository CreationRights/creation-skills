# **Section 1: The Problem**

## **Eyebrow**

THE PROBLEM

## **Headline**

Late rights review creates late-stage pressure.

## **Body**

When rights review happens at the end, teams are forced into difficult choices.

Delay the launch.  
Rush the review.  
Rework the asset.  
Ask legal to decide with incomplete context.  
Assume an old approval still applies.  
Move forward without a clear record.

None of those are ideal.

The better model is to place review triggers earlier, while the asset is still being prepared.

## **Supporting Line**

Rights review is easier before the asset is finished.

# **Section 2: Rights-Sensitive Moments Appear Early**

## **Eyebrow**

EARLY SIGNALS

## **Headline**

Most review issues are visible before final delivery.

## **Body**

The company can often see review needs early if the right triggers are built into the production path.

## **Early Review Signals**

A person appears in the asset.  
A creator deliverable is being reused.  
A model image is moving into a new market.  
A product claim appears in copy.  
A clip includes third-party material.  
A voice is being used.  
A sponsor association is added.  
A social post is being boosted.  
An old asset is being refreshed.  
A new territory or channel is being added.  
An agent prepares a version from previous material.

## **Outcome**

If the company can identify these signals early, the review can happen before the work moves too far.

# **Section 3: What Rights Review Should Cover**

## **Eyebrow**

REVIEW SCOPE

## **Headline**

Rights review is about use, source, identity, and permission.

## **Body**

Rights review should help the company understand whether the intended use is clear, approved, and recorded.

## **Review Areas**

### **Source Material**

Where the asset came from, who owns it, and whether third-party material, archive material, creator content, or licensed material is included.

### **Talent and NILP®**

Whether name, image, likeness, persona, voice, performance, or identifiable presence appears in the media.

### **Consent**

Whether permission exists for the intended use.

### **Usage Scope**

Whether duration, territory, channel, format, paid media, organic use, partner use, sponsor use, or reuse are covered.

### **Product Claims**

Whether claims, comparisons, endorsements, product benefits, technical statements, or legal-sensitive language need review.

### **Reuse**

Whether an asset approved once can move into a new context.

### **Records**

What needs to be captured before the asset moves forward.

# **Section 4: NILP® Checks Should Not Wait**

## **Eyebrow**

NILP®

## **Headline**

Identity-sensitive media needs earlier checks.

## **Body**

NILP® means **Name, Image, Likeness, and Persona**.

A NILP® check may be needed when media uses a person’s identity, likeness, voice, persona, creator content, performance, or recognizable presence.

## **NILP® Review May Be Needed When**

Name is used  
Image is used  
Likeness is used  
Persona is used  
Voice is used  
Talent is identifiable  
Creator content is included  
Athlete content is included  
Artist content is included  
Model imagery is used  
Employee content is used  
Customer testimonial is used  
Behind-the-scenes footage is included  
Paid media is planned  
Sponsor association is included  
Asset is reused  
Asset is localized  
Usage window changes  
New channel or territory is added

## **Supporting Line**

If identity is part of the media, review should happen before the use expands.

# **Section 5: Paid Media Changes the Question**

## **Eyebrow**

PAID MEDIA

## **Headline**

Organic approval is not always paid approval.

## **Body**

A common rights issue appears when organic content becomes paid media.

A creator post gets boosted.  
A social clip becomes an ad.  
A model image moves into paid campaign use.  
A product testimonial becomes performance media.  
An athlete moment becomes sponsor content.

The asset may have been approved for one use.

That does not mean it was approved for every use.

## **Paid Media Review Questions**

Was paid media included in the original approval?  
Was the usage window limited?  
Was the territory limited?  
Was the channel limited?  
Was whitelisting allowed?  
Was sponsor association allowed?  
Was the talent or creator identifiable?  
Was NILP® present?  
Was consent captured?  
Does the new use require additional approval?  
What record should exist?

## **Outcome**

Paid media review becomes a gate, not a late surprise.

# **Section 7: Production Agents Can Flag, Not Clear**

## **Eyebrow**

PRODUCTION AGENTS

## **Headline**

Agents can help surface rights questions earlier.

## **Body**

Production agents can help identify when review may be needed.

They can flag possible talent usage, NILP®, missing consent notes, product claims, reuse questions, paid media expansion, territory changes, channel changes, and incomplete records.

But agents should not clear rights.

## **Agents May Help Flag**

Talent likeness  
Voice use  
Creator content  
Third-party material  
Archive use  
Product claims  
Paid media use  
Sponsor use  
New territory  
New channel  
Usage window changes  
Asset reuse  
Localization  
Missing consent notes  
Incomplete rights status  
Missing approval record

## **Agents Must Not**

Approve talent usage  
Approve NILP® usage  
Change rights terms  
Clear product claims  
Approve paid media use  
Reuse restricted assets  
Bypass legal escalation  
Approve final delivery  
Make legal determinations

## **Core Line**

The agent does the prep.  
The human makes the call.  
The system records both.
