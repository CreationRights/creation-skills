# **Section 1: Why Operating Files Matter**

## **Eyebrow**

WHY THEY MATTER

## **Headline**

Agents cannot follow the company flow if the rules live in someone’s head.

## **Body**

A production agent is only as useful as the instructions around it.

If the rules are scattered across private prompts, chat histories, old decks, Slack messages, or individual habits, the company cannot reliably control the output.

Operating files make the company’s production rules visible.

They explain what the agent can do, what it cannot do, what examples it should follow, who owns the output, when a person approves, and what needs to be recorded before media moves forward.

# **Section 3: From Prompt to Operating File**

## **Eyebrow**

FROM PROMPT TO FILE

## **Headline**

A prompt is not enough for production.

## **Body**

A prompt can help someone get a useful output once.

An operating file helps the company get controlled outputs repeatedly.

The difference is ownership, structure, review, and limits.

## **Comparison**

### **Loose Prompt**

Written by an individual.  
Stored privately.  
Changed without review.  
May not include blocked actions.  
May not define approval.  
May not explain rights or reuse limits.  
May not leave a clear record.

### **Operating File**

Owned by the company.  
Attached to a defined agent role.  
Includes approved examples and blocked actions.  
Defines output format and naming rules.  
Includes human review steps.  
Connects to rights and approval triggers.  
Can be updated under owner control.

## **Supporting Line**

A prompt gets a result.  
An operating file creates a repeatable company practice.

# **Section 4: What Goes Inside**

## **Eyebrow**

FILE STRUCTURE

## **Headline**

Each operating file should make the task clear.

## **Body**

Creation Skills designs operating files around the production job, the company standard, and the approval path.

## **Core Sections**

### **Agent Role**

What the agent is for and where it sits in the production path.

### **Task Rules**

What the agent can support and what kind of output it should prepare.

### **Approved Examples**

Examples of work the company wants the agent to follow.

### **Blocked Actions**

Tasks the agent must not perform or decide.

### **Output Format**

The exact structure, file format, naming convention, or delivery style expected.

### **Review Steps**

Who reviews the output and when work can move forward.

### **Escalation Notes**

What the agent or owner should do when the output touches legal, rights, talent, NILP®, brand, or delivery risk.

### **Owner Instructions**

Who maintains the file, approves updates, and keeps the agent inside the company rules.

# **Section 6: Blocked Actions**

## **Eyebrow**

LIMITS

## **Headline**

Operating files should say what cannot happen.

## **Body**

Blocked actions are as important as approved tasks.

They keep agents from drifting into decisions they should not make.

## **Common Blocked Actions**

Cannot publish final media.  
Cannot approve talent usage.  
Cannot change rights terms.  
Cannot override brand review.  
Cannot approve product claims.  
Cannot bypass legal escalation.  
Cannot reuse restricted assets.  
Cannot approve NILP® usage.  
Cannot make final client-facing decisions.  
Cannot change operating files without owner approval.  
Cannot create new usage rights.  
Cannot approve final delivery.

## **Outcome**

Production agents become useful support without becoming uncontrolled decision makers.

# **Section 9: Operating Files and Learning**

## **Eyebrow**

LEARNING WITH CONTROL

## **Headline**

Operating files improve from approved work.

## **Body**

Operating files should evolve as the company learns.

Agents may suggest changes based on approved outputs, rejected drafts, final edits, producer notes, client feedback, delivery issues, and team corrections.

But nothing material should change on its own.

## **Learning Rule**

The agent can suggest an update.  
The owner approves it.  
The operating file changes.  
The next output improves.
