# **Section 1: Why This Matters**

## **Eyebrow**

WHY IT MATTERS

## **Headline**

Media creation often involves sensitive material before anyone calls it sensitive.

## **Body**

The materials behind modern media can carry legal, commercial, creative, talent, privacy, and reputation risk.

A rough cut may include unreleased footage.  
A caption file may reveal a campaign.  
A talent note may include usage limits.  
A contract may contain rights terms.  
A source file may include private metadata.  
A creator asset may include NILP® or consent issues.  
A product claim may need legal review.  
An operating file may reveal internal production rules.

Creation Skills helps companies review the system around those materials while keeping the materials protected through the right client process.

# **Section 2: What to Share First**

## **Eyebrow**

FIRST CONTACT

## **Headline**

Describe the company flow before sending files.

## **Body**

For the first message, Creation Skills usually needs context, not sensitive assets.

## **Safe First-Contact Information**

Company type  
Team type  
Production path to review  
General issue  
Teams involved  
Tools or systems involved  
Whether production agents are already in use  
Where approval is unclear  
Where rights review arrives late  
Whether Creation Rights adoption is being considered  
What outcome the company wants

## **Example**

Instead of sending unreleased campaign files, write:

“Our social team is using agents to prepare cutdown options from long-form video. Approval is unclear when talent appears, and paid media reuse is being discussed after the first version is already made.”

## **Outcome**

Creation Skills can understand the issue and decide the right next step without receiving sensitive materials too early.

# **Section 9: Production Agents and Access**

## **Eyebrow**

PRODUCTION AGENTS

## **Headline**

Agent access should not be granted casually.

## **Body**

If a company uses production agents, Creation Skills may review how those agents are structured.

That does not mean agents should be given open access to sensitive systems or assets.

## **Agent Access Questions**

What files can the agent access?  
What systems can the agent touch?  
Can the agent read metadata?  
Can the agent suggest metadata?  
Can the agent access talent materials?  
Can the agent access rights notes?  
Can the agent access unreleased assets?  
Can the agent prepare public-facing outputs?  
Who reviews the output?  
What record is kept?

## **Core Line**

The agent does the prep.  
The human makes the call.  
The system records both.

# **Section 10: Client Systems and Credentials**

## **Eyebrow**

ACCESS AND SECURITY

## **Headline**

Never send credentials through forms or email.

## **Body**

Creation Skills should not receive credentials, passwords, access tokens, private keys, admin logins, open-access links, or system secrets through public forms or general email.

## **Do Not Send**

Passwords  
API keys  
Access tokens  
Private keys  
Admin credentials  
Shared login details  
Open-access asset links  
Unprotected download folders  
Private system URLs with credentials  
Internal access instructions

## **Safer Approach**

If access is needed, it should be handled through the client’s approved access process with proper permissions, scope, and review.

# **Section 12: Partner and Third-Party Materials**

## **Eyebrow**

THIRD PARTIES

## **Headline**

Partner materials may require permission before review.

## **Body**

Many companies work with agencies, production companies, creators, talent representatives, legal advisors, localization vendors, DAM partners, and platform teams.

Materials created by or for third parties may have sharing limits.

## **Before Sharing Partner Materials**

Confirm ownership.  
Confirm sharing rights.  
Remove unnecessary sensitive details.  
Redact private information where possible.  
Check whether client, talent, agency, or partner approval is required.  
Use a representative example when enough.  
Share only what is needed for the agreed scope.

## **Outcome**

The review can move forward without over-sharing materials that belong to another party or require additional approval.
