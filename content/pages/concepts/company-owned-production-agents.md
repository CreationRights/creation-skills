# **Section 1: Why Company-Owned Agents**

## **Eyebrow**

WHY IT MATTERS

## **Headline**

Useful agents become risky when the company does not own the rules.

## **Body**

Many teams already have people using agents inside production.

A producer may use an agent to summarize footage.  
A social team may use one to draft captions.  
A localization team may use one to prepare translation drafts.  
A product team may use one to generate asset versions.  
An editor may use one to organize notes.  
A campaign team may use one to package delivery materials.

The issue is not the use of agents.

The issue is whether the company owns the way those agents are used.

Who owns the agent?  
What files can it access?  
What tasks are approved?  
What actions are blocked?  
Who reviews the output?  
What triggers legal, brand, talent, or rights review?  
What record is kept before media moves forward?

Creation Skills helps companies answer those questions.

# **Section 2: What Makes an Agent Company-Owned**

## **Eyebrow**

THE MODEL

## **Headline**

A production agent needs a job, owner, file, limit, and approval path.

## **Body**

A company-owned production agent is not just a chatbot with a good prompt.

It is a governed production support inside the company system.

## **Agent Requirements**

### **Defined Role**

The agent has a specific production job and does not operate outside that job.

### **Named Owner**

A person or team is responsible for reviewing outputs, updating operating files, and approving changes.

### **Approved Tasks**

The agent knows what tasks it can support.

### **Blocked Actions**

The agent knows what it must never do.

### **Operating Files**

The agent follows company-owned instructions, examples, formats, naming rules, and review steps.

### **Human Approval Path**

A person reviews the outputs before the work moves into sensitive, public, client-facing, paid, reused, or final contexts.

### **Risk Level**

The company understands whether the agent supports low, medium, or high-risk production activity.

### **Review Record**

The company can explain what the agent prepared, who reviewed it, and what moved forward.

# **Section 4: Example Agent**

## **Eyebrow**

EXAMPLE

## **Headline**

Shorts Production Agent

## **Body**

A Shorts Production Agent supports the social or content team by preparing short-form options from longer video material.

## **Agent Profile Example**

**Role:** Turns long video notes into short clip suggestions, captions, hooks, and post formats.

**Owner:** Social Producer

**Department:** Social / Content

**Access:** Raw video notes, approved examples, campaign brief, platform format guide.

**Approved Tasks:** Clip suggestions, caption drafts, hook options, format notes, post structure options.

**Blocked Tasks:** Cannot publish. Cannot approve talent usage. Cannot change rights terms. Cannot override brand review. Cannot approve final cuts.

**Human Gate:** Producer approves all suggestions before editing or posting.

**Review Path:** Social producer, brand lead, legal or talent review where triggered.

**Learning Loop:** Improves from approved cuts, rejected hooks, final captions, and team feedback.

**Risk Level:** Medium

# **Section 9: Learning With Control**

## **Eyebrow**

LEARNING WITH CONTROL

## **Headline**

Agents improve because the team teaches them.

## **Body**

Production agents should improve from approved work, not uncontrolled self-change.

Agents can learn from final edits, rejected drafts, approved cuts, preferred captions, producer notes, client feedback, delivery results, and team corrections.

Nothing important should change on its own.

## **Learning Rule**

The agent can suggest an update.  
The owner approves it.  
The operating file changes.  
The next output improves.

## **Learning Signals**

# **Section 10: System Repair**

## **Eyebrow**

SYSTEM REPAIR

## **Headline**

Agents can help catch breaks before work gets lost.

## **Body**

Production often breaks in small places.

Missing files.  
Wrong formats.  
Unclear owners.  
Late reviews.  
Old briefs.  
Incomplete asset packages.  
Agent outputs outside the task.  
Rights checks that happen too late.

Creation Skills defines where agents can flag those issues and route work back to the right owner.

## **Repair Examples**

### **Missing source file**

Route back to producer.

### **Wrong export format**

Return to production owner.

### **Caption file missing**

Notify social or accessibility lead.

### **Asset package incomplete**

Block delivery checklist.

### **Brief changed after production started**

Flag affected agents and review owners.

### **Agent exceeds task limit**

Pause the agent and notify owner.

### **Rights check incomplete**

Block movement into approval or delivery.
