# **Section 1: Core Language**

## **Eyebrow**

CORE TERMS

## **Headline**

The terms that define Creation Skills.

## **Term Cards**

### **Company Flow**

The way creative work moves through the company.

This includes people, files, production agents, review steps, approval gates, rights checks, delivery, reuse, and records.

### **Production Path**

A repeatable path for making a specific kind of media.

Examples include campaign production, product media, release assets, episode packaging, social cutdowns, localization, talent-led content, archive refresh, or final delivery packages.

### **Governed Media Creation**

A media creation model where people, agents, approvals, rights checks, and records work together with clear control.

The goal is not slower production. The goal is controlled production.

### **Creation Rights Readiness**

The company’s level of preparation before Creation Rights carries the protected layer.

Readiness includes clear owners, production paths, approval gates, agent rules, operating files, rights review points, metadata needs, and records.

# **Section 2: Agent Language**

## **Eyebrow**

PRODUCTION AGENTS

## **Headline**

The language we use for agent-supported production.

## **Term Cards**

### **Company-Owned Production Agent**

A production agent configured around the company’s actual media tasks, examples, limits, files, review paths, and approval rules.

It belongs to the company, not to one person’s private prompt habit.

### **Agent Owner**

The person or team responsible for a production agent.

The owner reviews outputs, approves updates, maintains operating files, and makes sure the agent stays inside its limits.

### **Approved Tasks**

The tasks a production agent is allowed to support.

Examples include caption drafts, clip suggestions, product copy drafts, translation drafts, asset package checks, metadata preparation, or delivery checklists.

### **Blocked Actions**

The actions a production agent must not perform.

Examples include publishing final media, approving talent usage, changing rights terms, approving product claims, bypassing legal review, or changing operating files without owner approval.

### **Human Checkpoint**

A point where a person reviews agent-prepared work before it moves forward.

Human checkpoints are part of the approval model.

### **Learning Loop**

The controlled process where agents improve from approved work, rejected drafts, final edits, owner feedback, and team corrections.

The agent can suggest an update.  
The owner approves it.  
The operating file changes.  
The next output improves.

# **Section 3: Operating Language**

## **Eyebrow**

OPERATING FILES

## **Headline**

The files that turn scattered instructions into company rules.

## **Term Cards**

### **Operating File**

A company-owned instruction file that defines how a production agent or team should handle a specific production task.

It can include approved tasks, blocked actions, examples, formats, naming rules, review steps, escalation notes, owner instructions, and risk level.

### **Prompt Library**

A structured set of reusable prompts connected to approved tasks, operating files, output formats, examples, and review steps.

A prompt library should belong to the company.

### **Approved Example**

A piece of work the company wants agents and teams to learn from.

Examples may include final captions, approved campaign copy, product descriptions, edit notes, delivery packages, or market versions.

### **Rejected Example**

A piece of work the company does not want repeated.

Rejected examples help agents and teams understand what not to produce.

### **Owner Instruction**

A rule inside an operating file that explains who maintains the file, who approves changes, and when the file should be reviewed.

# **Section 4: Approval Language**

## **Eyebrow**

HUMAN APPROVAL

## **Headline**

People approve the moments that matter.

## **Term Cards**

### **Human Approval Gate**

A defined decision point where a person reviews work before it can move forward.

It shows who reviews, who approves, what triggers review, what blocks movement, what record is required, and where escalation goes.

### **Approval Path**

The order of review for a specific production path.

It may include creative, brand, legal, rights, talent, product, platform, client, or final delivery review.

### **Final Sign-Off**

The final human approval before media moves into publication, paid media, client delivery, partner delivery, reuse, or another controlled context.

### **Escalation**

The process for routing unclear, incomplete, risky, disputed, or sensitive work to the right owner before it moves forward.

### **Exception**

A case that does not fit the normal rule set.

An exception should be paused, routed, reviewed, decided, and recorded.

## **Core Line**

The agent does the prep.  
The human makes the call.  
The system records both.

# **Section 5: Rights and Talent Language**

## **Eyebrow**

RIGHTS AND TALENT

## **Headline**

Rights-sensitive media needs earlier review.

## **Term Cards**

### **NILP®**

Name, Image, Likeness, and Persona.

NILP® review matters when a person’s identity, likeness, voice, persona, or related identity signals are used in media.

### **Talent Usage**

The use of a person, performer, creator, model, athlete, artist, employee, subject, voice, likeness, or persona inside media.

### **Consent Review**

The review of whether a person’s permission or usage terms are present, clear, current, and sufficient for the intended use.

### **Usage Scope**

The permitted use of an asset.

Usage scope may include duration, territory, channel, format, paid media, organic social, partner use, client use, exclusivity, and reuse.

### **Rights Review Trigger**

A condition that requires rights review before media moves forward.

Examples include talent likeness, voice, creator content, third-party material, paid media, new territory, new channel, extended usage window, or asset reuse.

### **Reuse Control**

A rule that decides when an existing asset can be cut down, localized, repackaged, translated, refreshed, boosted, or moved into a new channel.

# **Section 6: Asset and Record Language**

## **Eyebrow**

ASSETS AND RECORDS

## **Headline**

Finished media needs more than files.

## **Term Cards**

### **Asset Lifecycle**

The full life of a media asset from creation to review, approval, delivery, reuse, refresh, archive, retirement, or protected record.

### **Delivery Package**

The final collection of files, formats, captions, metadata, approval notes, rights notes, and delivery requirements needed before media moves forward.

### **Protected Record**

A record that should be governed because it touches rights, NILP®, consent, approvals, metadata, audit, payouts, or compliance-sensitive media activity.

### **Metadata Requirement**

The information that must be captured around an asset before it can move forward.

This may include owner, approver, rights status, NILP® status, consent status, usage scope, territory, channel, format, version, reuse status, and audit reference.

### **Audit Reference**

A record that helps explain what happened, who approved it, why it moved, and what status applied at the time.

### **Platform Entry Point**

The point where media, approvals, rights notes, metadata, or protected records are ready to enter Creation Rights or another controlled system.
